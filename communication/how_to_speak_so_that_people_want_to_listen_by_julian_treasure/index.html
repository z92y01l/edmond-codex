How to Speak so that People Want to Listen

1. The Power of the Voice​

The human voice is described as the most powerful sound in the world—the only one that can start a war or say "I love you". To speak powerfully and make change, we must first ensure people listen.

2. The Seven Deadly Sins of Speaking (Habits to Avoid)​

Treasure identifies seven common speaking habits that prevent people from listening:

  1. Gossip: Speaking ill of someone not present. People know the gossiper will eventually gossip about them.
  2. Judging: It's difficult to listen to someone if you feel you are being judged and found wanting.
  3. Negativity: Being constantly negative or complaining is "viral misery" and makes one difficult to listen to.
  4. Excuses: People who constantly shift blame onto others and fail to take responsibility are hard to listen to.
  5. Embroidery/Exaggeration/Lying: Exaggeration demeans language. Lying makes people stop listening entirely.
  6. Dogmatism: Confusing facts with opinions and bombarding listeners with opinions presented as truth.

3. The Four Cornerstones of Powerful Speech (The Word "HAIL")​

To make your speech powerful and effective, Treasure suggests building it on four foundations that spell the word HAIL (defined as "to greet or acclaim enthusiastically"):

  • H - Honesty: Being true, straight, and clear in what you say.
  • A - Authenticity: Just being yourself, or "standing in your own truth".
  • I - Integrity: Being your word, doing what you say, and being someone people can trust
  • L - Love: Wishing people well. This tempers honesty (e.g., preventing unnecessary rudeness) and makes it difficult to judge others simultaneously.

4. The Speaker's Vocal Toolbox​

Beyond what you say, the way you say it is crucial. Treasure highlights seven vocal tools to improve speaking power:

  1. Register (Pitch): Locating your voice in the chest provides weight, power, and authority. The research suggests people vote for politicians with lower voices.
  2. Timbre: The way your voice feels. Rich, smooth, warm voices are preferred (like "hot chocolate"). Timbre can be improved through training, breathing, and posture.
  3. Prosody: The "sing-song" or metalanguage used to impart meaning. Speaking on one note (monotonic) is hard to listen to.
  4. Pace: Speaking quickly to indicate excitement or slowing down to emphasize a point. At the extreme end of slowing down is the use of silence.
  5. Silence: A moment of quiet can be very powerful and should not be filled with "ums" and "ahs".
  6. Pitch (for arousal): Often used with pace to indicate arousal (excitement or anxiety) or to imply different meanings in a question.
  7. Volume: Using volume variation to indicate excitement (loud) or to draw attention (quiet). Avoid "sod-casting," which is broadcasting loudly and inconsiderately all the time.

5. Warming Up the Engine​

Before any important conversation or presentation, you must warm up your voice. Treasure provides six vocal warm-up exercises:

  1. Sigh Out: Deep breath in, arms up, and sigh out.
  2. Lips: "Bumble" your lips with sound (e.g., "b-b-b-b-b").
  3. Tongue: Exaggerated tongue articulation (e.g., "la-la-la-la-la").
  4. Rolling 'R's: Rolling your "R"s
  5. The Siren (Pros' Favorite): Start high with "wee" and slide down to a low "aw" (Wee-aw-wee-aw).

Conclusion​

The speaker envisions a world where people speak powerfully (with HAIL and vocal tools) to conscious listeners in environments designed for sound, leading to a world where "understanding would be the norm".
